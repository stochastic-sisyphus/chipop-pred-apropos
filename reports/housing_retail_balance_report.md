 # Housing-Retail Balance Analysis Report

## Executive Summary

This report analyzes the balance between housing development and retail licenses across Chicago ZIP codes, with a focus on identifying areas that are experiencing new housing development without corresponding retail growth. Such areas may represent opportunities for targeted economic development and strategic planning to ensure balanced community development.

Key findings include:

1. Several Chicago ZIP codes are experiencing significant new multi-family housing development that had little to no such development in previous years.
2. Areas with high housing-to-retail ratios may face retail amenity gaps that could impact quality of life for residents.
3. The housing-retail balance varies significantly between downtown and non-downtown areas, with different patterns of development.
4. Strategic retail development opportunities exist in specific ZIP codes that have seen housing growth outpace retail establishment growth.
5. Analysis of 10-year permit trends reveals ZIP codes with sustained growth of at least 20%, highlighting long-term development patterns.

## Methodology

The analysis used the following data sources:
- Chicago building permits data (2015-2025)
- Chicago business license data (2015-2025)
- Population and demographic patterns data

We identified several key metrics for analysis:
1. **Emerging Housing Areas**: ZIP codes with significant recent housing development and/or multi-family construction that had minimal such activity in the past.
2. **Retail Deficit Areas**: Areas where housing permit activity substantially exceeds retail license activity.
3. **New Multi-Family Areas**: ZIP codes that had minimal multi-family development historically but have seen significant recent growth.
4. **10-Year Permit Growth**: ZIP codes that have experienced at least 20% growth in permit activity over the past decade, comparing the first half (2015-2019) with the second half (2020-2025).

### 3. Downtown vs. Non-Downtown Comparison

Our analysis revealed significant differences in housing-retail balance between downtown and non-downtown areas:

- **Downtown**: 668 housing permits vs. 1,459 retail licenses (218.4% retail-to-housing ratio)
- **Non-Downtown**: 2,612 housing permits vs. 9,689 retail licenses (370.9% retail-to-housing ratio)

Downtown areas have a more balanced mix of housing and retail, while non-downtown areas show greater variation, with some areas having substantial retail gaps.

## Strategic Implications

### Retail Development Opportunities

The analysis highlights several ZIP codes that represent strategic opportunities for retail development:

1. **South Side Emerging Areas**: ZIP codes 60617, 60649, and 60619 show significant new housing development but limited retail growth.

2. **West Side Growth Areas**: ZIP codes 60644 and 60624 have emerging multi-family development that may benefit from targeted retail initiatives.

3. **Areas with High Housing-Retail Imbalance**: ZIP codes 60652, 60620, and 60643 show substantial housing development with limited corresponding retail establishment growth.

4. **Long-Term Growth Areas**: ZIP codes 60653, 60608, and 60651 demonstrate sustained growth over a decade and may require comprehensive retail planning to meet future demand.

### Policy Recommendations

Based on the analysis, we recommend the following policy approaches:

1. **Targeted Retail Incentives**: Develop specific incentive programs for retail businesses in identified high-deficit areas.

2. **Mixed-Use Development Requirements**: Consider zoning changes that encourage or require ground-floor retail in new multi-family developments in emerging areas.

3. **Community Amenity Planning**: Work with community organizations to identify specific retail gaps (grocery stores, restaurants, etc.) in emerging housing areas.

4. **Retail Corridor Development**: Establish or strengthen existing retail corridors in areas with new multi-family development to create walkable neighborhood centers.

5. **Small Business Support**: Provide technical assistance and financing support for small businesses looking to establish in retail deficit areas.

6. **Long-Term Growth Planning**: For areas showing consistent growth over the 10-year period, develop comprehensive retail development strategies that anticipate continued housing growth.

## Conclusion

Chicago is experiencing significant housing development patterns that vary considerably across different neighborhoods. By identifying areas with new housing growth but limited retail development, this analysis provides a foundation for targeted economic development strategies that can help create more balanced, livable communities throughout the city.

The data suggests particular attention should be paid to specific South and West side neighborhoods that are seeing new housing investment but may lack corresponding retail amenities. Additionally, areas with sustained growth over the past decade highlight neighborhoods undergoing significant transformation that will require careful planning to maintain housing-retail balance.

Addressing these imbalances can help ensure that housing development translates into true neighborhood improvement and increased quality of life for residents.
